⑤ · Step 1
Step 1 — Pick the Problem, Not the Tool
Start with the pain.
Do not choose tools first. Choose the job to be done first.

Typical PMO problems worth starting with:
Portfolio visibility
Executive reporting inconsistency
Meeting overload
Risk detection
Status reporting burden
Demand intake
The principle
"If you start with the tool, you will find problems that fit the tool — not the problems that matter most."
The question is not "What can AI do for us?"
The question is "What problem costs us the most — and what would solving it actually change?"

⑥ · Step 2
Step 2 — Match the Right AI Pattern to the Problem
Repetitive process with clear rules
→
RPA
Access or interaction pain
→
Conversational AI
Creation, synthesis, summarization
→
Generative AI
Multi-step coordination across systems
→
Agentic AI
The type of pain should determine the type of AI. Not every problem needs a bazooka.

⑦ · Step 3
Step 3 — Check Readiness Honestly
Readiness is not binary.
It is a profile. Most organizations underestimate readiness gaps until they are 3 months into a failing pilot.
Readiness kills more AI initiatives than technology does.
Process clarity
Are steps defined well enough for AI to follow?
Data quality
Is the data clean, available, structured?
Ownership
Is there a clear person accountable?
Governance
Are guardrails defined for AI outputs?
Sponsorship
Does leadership actively support this?
User willingness
Will people actually use it when it's live?

⑧ · Step 4
Step 4 — Pilot for Learning, Not Theater
✕ Bad pilot
"Let's try AI on status reports."
No hypothesis. No measurement. No timeframe.
Produces a demo. Tells you nothing useful.
Team celebrates the tool — not the outcome.
✓ Better pilot
"We believe AI-assisted summaries will reduce reporting time for PMs with high reporting load, and we will know in 6 weeks."
Concrete hypothesis. Defined success signal.
Creates learning — not just a nice presentation.
A pilot must prove a hypothesis — not produce a nice demo.
AI-4 · PMO unCON 2026
⑨ · Step 5
Step 5 — Scale Only What Changed Behavior
Do not scale because
the demo looked impressive.
Scale only when there is evidence of real change — not enthusiasm about the tool.
"If it only produced demos, do not scale it."
Scale only when
1
Behavior changed — not just tool adoption
2
The workflow improved measurably
3
Ownership is clear — someone is accountable
4
Value is visible to the business
5
People actually use it in their real work

Key Tensions for a Real Roadmap
Pilot vs. scale
Visible wins vs. foundations
Tool roadmap vs. capability roadmap
Ambition vs. execution discipline
Experiment vs. integration
A useful roadmap  manages these tensions instead of ignoring them.

⑪ · What Goes Wrong
What Most Organizations Get Wrong
They confuse activity
with progress.
Most organizations think they need a tool roadmap.

What they really need is a capability roadmap.
They start with the tool
They confuse activity with progress
They run pilots with no hypothesis
They spread ownership across too many people
They try to scale before behavior changes
They build an ideas list and call it a roadmap

⑫ · 90-Day Roadmap
A Practical 90-Day Roadmap
Short enough to force prioritization. Concrete enough to drive action.
Days 1–30
Discover
Map real PMO pain points. Assess data and process readiness. Identify sponsors and owners. Select 1–2 use cases to validate.
Days 31–60
Pilot
Run a narrow, hypothesis-driven pilot. Measure behavior change. Document what broke and why. Build governance layer early.
Days 61–90
Decide
Scale, stop, or pivot — explicitly. Define capability gaps to close. Set the next 90-day intention. Communicate results to executives.
A roadmap becomes real when decisions become explicit.

⑬ · Days 1–30
Days 1–30 · Discover
Narrow the field.
Don't collect more ideas.
The goal of the first 30 days is not to generate a longer list. It is to identify the one or two problems worth betting on.
What to do
Map real PMO pain points
Assess data and process readiness
Identify sponsors and owners
Select 1–2 use cases to validate
"The goal is to narrow the field."
AI-4 · PMO unCON 2026
⑭ · Days 31–60
Days 31–60 · Pilot
Create learning,
not theater.
A pilot is not a proof of concept. It is a structured test of a specific hypothesis with a defined success signal.
What to do
Run a narrow, hypothesis-driven pilot
Measure behavior change — not just adoption rate
Document what broke and why
Build the governance layer early
"A pilot should create learning, not theater."

⑮ · Days 61–90
Days 61–90 · Decide
Scale, stop, or pivot —
explicitly.
Most organizations avoid the decision. They let mediocre pilots run indefinitely. A real roadmap forces an explicit choice.
What to do
Scale, stop, or pivot — explicitly
Define capability gaps to close
Set the next 90-day intention
Communicate results to executives
"A roadmap becomes real when decisions become explicit."

⑯ · Per Use Case
For Each Use Case — Define Five Elements
01 · What
What is the use case?
02 · Why
Why does it matter to the business?
03 · Gap
What readiness gap must be closed first?
04 · Owner
Who is accountable for this use case?
05 · Signal
What success signal will tell us it worked?
If these five are not clear, the roadmap is not mature yet.

⑰ · Wins & Foundations
Quick Wins and Foundations
Some wins should be
visible fast.
Others make scale possible later. A practical roadmap needs both.
Do not confuse early visibility with long-term readiness.
Quick win
AI-assisted status summaries
Visible, fast, immediate value. PMs feel the difference in week one.
Foundation
Standard project metadata & cleaner portfolio taxonomy
Invisible now. Makes everything that follows actually work at scale.

⑱ · PMO Role
What the PMO Must Own
The PMO doesn't need to
build everything.
But it must own the right things — the decisions, not just the deployments.
The better question: "How can AI help us create better decisions, clearer visibility, and services people actually value?"
The PMO must help own
Prioritization criteria
Governance
Adoption
Connection to value
Do not ask only "how AI can help us work faster."
