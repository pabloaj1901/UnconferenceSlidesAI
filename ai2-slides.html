Q1 · AI Readiness Assessment
Q1 — What does "AI readiness"
look like — honestly?
Readiness is not whether you have a Copilot license. It's not whether your CIO approved an AI strategy. It's not whether your team completed an online course.

AI readiness is an honest answer to four questions — and most PMOs find they're in a very different place than they assumed.
→
Score your PMO 1–10 on each of the four dimensions.
→
Key question: which of the four dimensions is your biggest gap — and what's actually driving it?

③ · 0:30 · 25 min
The 5 dimensions of AI readiness
Dimension 01
Data Maturity
Is our project data clean enough to trust? Not perfect — usable. The foundation everything else depends on.
"Not perfect data. Usable data."
Dimension 02
Process Clarity
Are PMO processes defined clearly enough that AI could follow them? Chaos accelerated is still chaos.
"If it's chaotic, AI only accelerates the chaos."
Dimension 03
People Confidence
Do people have baseline literacy — and psychological safety — to engage with AI without shutting down?
"Curiosity is not capability."
Dimension 04
Leadership Alignment
Has leadership actually defined what "AI-enabled PMO" means for this organisation — or is it a vague directive?
"Executive excitement may open the door. Operational reality decides whether AI stays."
Dimension 05
Governance & Trust
Enough trust to adopt. Enough guardrails not to stall. Both are required.
"Governance without usability kills momentum. Momentum without governance kills trust."
Most PMOs are strong on 2 dimensions and genuinely weak on the other 3. The honest assessment — not the aspirational one — is the only useful starting point.

Q2 · Roles & Skills
Q2 — Which skills become more valuable — and which less?
Doubling Down →
Skills becoming more valuable
→Evaluating AI output critically — knowing when it's confidently wrong
→Translating AI insight into something a nervous executive can act on
→Political navigation and stakeholder judgment
→Sitting with a team that is anxious about change
AI exposes these as irreplaceable.
Evolving Beyond →
Skills becoming less differentiating
→Writing status reports and compiling schedules
→Populating risk registers from interviews
→First-draft documentation and meeting summaries
→Routine data aggregation and formatting
Not worthless. Less differentiating.

Dynamic 1 — Q3 · Incremental Adoption
Incremental Adoption
The fastest path to failure
is announcing a transformation.
The fastest path to success is quietly solving a real problem. Not transformation. Infiltration — thoughtful, ethical, deliberately quiet infiltration of AI into work that already needs doing.
1
12 min: Design a 30-day micro-pilot. Four sentences maximum: one use case · one team · one success metric · one thing that must NOT be automated.
2
Swap your pilot design with the adjacent table. They prepare one stress-test question: "What happens if the AI output is wrong on day one?"
3
Each table responds in 2 min, then harvest — one thing per table, 30 seconds each.
Deliverable: A pilot specific enough to fail gracefully — and teach you something.
30-Day Pilot — Four Sentences
Use Case
What specific work will AI touch?
Team
Which specific team?
Metric
How do you know in 30 days?
NOT Auto
What must stay human?
"A good pilot is so specific it's almost embarrassing."

Q4 · Executive Buy-In
Executives don't fund AI projects.
Q4 — They fund solutions to problems they already have.
The Opening Move
Lead with the problem — never the capability
"AI can analyse our project data" is how you lose. "We keep getting surprised by project crises the PMO should have seen coming" is how you start.
"The technology is never the opening move. The problem is."
What Executives Fund
Their problem — in their language
Programmes failing to land outcomes · portfolio outpacing execution capacity · being surprised by crises. Connect AI to the problem that already keeps them up at night.
The Right Ask
Smallest possible proof point
One pilot · one number · one decision made faster. A 3-year AI transformation is not where you start. Prove small, then come back with evidence.
"Prove it small, then ask for more."

⑧ · 2:25 · Dynamic 2 · 35 min
Dynamic 2 — Executive Pressure
Do not pitch
the coolest idea.
Pitch the most defensible one. 90 seconds. Your CEO. Go.
1
12 min: Prepare a 90-second pitch. Nominate your spokesperson.
2
Each table delivers. Strictly 90 seconds. Room votes: most credible vs. "AI theatre."
3
Debrief: What made the winner credible? What was missing from the others?
Pitches that work lead with a named problem, a number, and an ask small enough to say yes to today.
Your 90-second pitch must include
Problem
One specific business problem AI will help solve — in their language
Number
One concrete number — even a small one
Risk
One honest risk you are asking them to accept
The Ask
One thing you are asking them to do right now
90 seconds · hard stop · the cut is part of the learning

Q5 · From Awareness to Action
Q5 — What will you personally do
by next Monday?
The organisations that will lead on AI are not the ones that planned the most. They are the ones that started first and learned fastest.

Not a project. Not a strategy. Not something that requires approval from three people. Something you can do alone, in an hour or less.
→
Write your three things privately first, then share with the table.
→
Table job: make sure each action is specific enough to be accountable. "Explore AI tools" is not specific enough.
Your index card — three things
01
The specific thing you will do by next Monday
Specific enough that you'd be embarrassed not to have done it. One hour or less. Alone.
02
The one thing you are still genuinely not sure about
Your honest uncertainty — one that sits in the middle of your AI journey without a clean answer.
03
Your biggest blocker
The one thing most likely to stop you from acting. Name it — structural or personal.
